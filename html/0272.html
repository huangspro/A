HiRT 2016

Study characteristics

Methods

  • Study design: single‐centre, open‐label, RCT

  • Study duration: recruited between 15 August 2012, and 20 March 2014, all of whom underwent transplant and were successfully followed at our centre through 18 June 2014

  • Study follow‐up period: 1 year

Participants

  • Country: USA

  • Setting: University of California San Francisco Medical Center

  • Inclusion criteria: adult patients with a diagnosis of diabetes mellitus who were admitted for deceased donor kidney transplantation

  • Number: treatment group (30); control group (30)

  • Mean age ± SD (years): treatment group (60.9 ± 9.1); control group (60.7 ± 11)

  • Sex: (M/F): treatment group (20/10); control group (20/10)

  • Exclusion criteria: children and adult candidates enrolled in a concurrent study evaluating the effect of a medication or other intervention on graft function

Interventions

Treatment group

  • Moderate intense glucose control

    • Insulin infusion if blood glucose > 6.7 mmol/L. To avoid prolonged treatment, the infusion was started no earlier than four hours before the anticipated start of the transplant

    • Intra‐operatively, blood glucose checked every 30 to 45 minutes with the goal of keeping it between 4.4 to 8.9 mmol/L

    • Use of bolus therapy or insulin infusion was left to the discretion of the anaesthesia team

    • Postoperatively, all recipients were placed on an insulin infusion with a target blood glucose level of 5.5 to 10 mmol/L for 24 hours postoperatively

    • After 24 hours of the infusion, the primary transplant team was given complete control over glucose management

Control group

  • Standard glucose control

    • Standard insulin sliding scale when serum blood glucose > 11.1 mmol/L perioperatively, at time of reperfusion, and postoperatively for 24 hours

    • After 24 hours, the primary transplant team was given complete control of glucose management and allowed to start insulin as they deemed appropriate

Outcomes

Outcomes from the study registry

  • Primary outcomes

    • Poor initial graft function defined by the occurrence of DGF (defined by a decrease in SCr < 10%/day for 3 consecutive days after transplant) or slow graft function (SCR > 3 mg/dL 5 days after transplant without dialysis)

    • Acute rejection at 90 days and graft survival/kidney function at 3 months, 6 months and then yearly

  • Secondary outcomes

    • Wound infection

    • Length of hospital stay

    • Death at 30 days.

    • Hypoglycaemic episodes (glucose < 70 mg/dL or < 3.9 mmol/L)

    • Stroke

Outcomes for this systematic review

  • Primary outcomes

    • Transplant or graft survival: Biopsy proven rejection and graft loss

    • DGF/dialysis within 7 days: poor graft function defined as need for dialysis within 7 days of transplant or failure of the SCr to drop by > 10% for 3 consecutive days. DGF (need for dialysis within 7 days of transplant)

    • HbA1c: not reported

    • FBG: not reported

    • Kidney function markers (including creatinine, eGFR, albuminuria): not reported

  • Secondary outcomes

    • Death (any cause): perioperative death.

    • Macrovascular events (cardiovascular death, non‐fatal MI, non‐fatal stroke, heart failure, peripheral vascular disease): stroke

    • Microvascular events (new or worsening kidney disease, doubling of SCr or retinopathy): not reported

    • Systolic and diastolic BP: not reported.

    • Lipids (HDL, LDL, TG): not reported

    • Body weight: not reported

    • Safety/adverse events (including hypoglycaemia): the primary safety measure was the number of severe hypoglycaemic events (blood glucose < 2.2 mmol/L). Also reported seizures.

    • QOL: not reported

Notes

  • Funding source: not reported

Risk of bias

Bias

Authors' judgement

Support for judgement

Random sequence generation (selection bias)

Low risk

Block randomisation strategy (block size of four) to ensure equal distribution of patients between study arms was used

Quote: “The randomisation was generated per computer algorithm.”

Allocation concealment (selection bias)

Low risk

Quote: “The randomisation scheme was hidden in an electronic file until each patient was enrolled.”

Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias)
All outcomes

High risk

The study was a “single‐center, open‐ label, randomised controlled trial comparing standard and moderately intense glucose control.”

Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)
All outcomes

High risk

The study was a “single‐center, open‐ label, randomised controlled trial comparing standard and moderately intense glucose control.”

Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)
All outcomes

Low risk

There were no drop‐outs from either study group

Selective reporting (reporting bias)

High risk

In the manuscript, the primary outcome was “primary outcome was poor graft function (dialysis within seven days of transplant or failure of serum creatinine to fall by 10% for three consecutive days).”

In the ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT01643382), the poor graft function was defined as “Our primary endpoint will be poor initial graft function defined by the occurrence of DGF (defined by a decrease in serum creatinine of <10%/day for 3 consecutive days after transplant) or slow graft function (serum creatinine >3 mg/dL 5 days after transplant without dialysis)”
